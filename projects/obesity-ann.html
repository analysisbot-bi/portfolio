Obesity Level Classification Using Artificial Neural Networks

A deep-learning study that predicts a person's obesity category from lifestyle and body-metric data — comparing three neural-network architectures to find the sweet spot between accuracy and generalisation.

ANNDeep LearningMulti-class Classification DropoutL2 RegularisationFeature Engineering PythonModel Evaluation

The problem

Obesity is rarely about a single number — it's the product of diet, activity, family history, and daily habits all tangled together. The goal here was to take that messy, multi-factor picture and train a model that could reliably sort a person into one of seven obesity categories, from underweight through to the different classes of obesity.

My approach

I treated this as a supervised multi-class classification problem and leaned on artificial neural networks. Before any modelling, I put real care into the data:

  • One-hot encoding for categorical lifestyle features so the network could read them cleanly.
  • MinMax scaling on numeric features to keep gradients well-behaved during training.
  • A careful train/test split so every accuracy number reflected genuinely unseen data.

Then I built and compared three ANN architectures to understand the trade-offs rather than just chasing one lucky run:

  • Baseline ANN — a simple network to establish an honest starting point.
  • Deep ANN — more layers and capacity to capture non-linear patterns.
  • Regularised ANN — the deep model plus dropout and L2 regularisation to fight overfitting.

What I found

The regularised network was the clear winner, reaching 87% test accuracy. More importantly, it generalised: the gap between training and validation performance stayed tight. I dug into confusion matrices to see exactly which categories got confused with their neighbours, and read the learning curves to confirm the model was learning signal, not noise.

What I took away

Raw depth isn't the answer — disciplined regularisation is what turned a decent model into a dependable one. Just as valuable was the habit of interpreting results (confusion matrices, learning curves) instead of trusting a single accuracy figure. That mindset carries into every project I build.
