Predicting Dog-Bite Incidents with Machine Learning for Big Data

Turning 11,688 real NYC health records into a predictive model that helps public-health teams anticipate when and where bite incidents are most likely — so resources can be planned proactively, not reactively.

Big DataRandom ForestFeature Engineering ClassificationPublic HealthPython Data VisualizationModel Comparison

The problem

Dog bites are a genuine public-health cost — emergency visits, follow-ups, and preventable harm. New York City's DOHMH publishes years of incident data, but a spreadsheet with tens of thousands of rows doesn't help a planner decide where to focus. The question I set out to answer: can we predict bite frequency well enough to support smarter resource allocation?

My approach

I worked with the full dataset of 11,688 DOHMH dog-bite records, starting with the unglamorous-but-essential work of cleaning and structuring the data. From there I focused on feature engineering:

  • Temporal features — extracting seasonality and time-of-year patterns from incident dates.
  • Demographic and location signals — encoding the contextual factors around each report.
  • Handling missing and inconsistent entries so the models trained on trustworthy inputs.

I then built and evaluated four machine-learning models head-to-head, comparing them on predictive accuracy rather than betting on a single algorithm.

What I found

Random Forest came out on top, delivering the highest accuracy for predicting bite frequency. Its ensemble nature handled the mix of engineered temporal and demographic features gracefully. I paired the model with clear visualisations that surfaced the patterns behind the predictions — the part that actually lets a public-health team act.

Why it matters

The output isn't just a metric; it's decision support. Anticipating higher-risk periods means outreach, education, and resources can be scheduled ahead of time. This project sharpened my instinct for connecting a model back to the real-world action it's meant to inform.
